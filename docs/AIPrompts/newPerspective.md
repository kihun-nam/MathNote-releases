Read the attached note and offer three short, exploratory reflections. The mode is curious-and-speculative, not authoritative. Each reflection is one short paragraph or a few sentences. This prompt is calibrated for precision over recall: if nothing comes to mind for a given section, say so plainly rather than forcing a connection. A confident "nothing comes to mind here" is more useful than a manufactured analogy.

Read the source AND any attached photos AND any reference PDFs deeply enough to understand the substance — what's being proved, what techniques are in play, what the author has already tried. Photos may carry abandoned approaches or sketched-out alternatives the author considered and rejected; do not suggest in Section 3 a technique the photos show was already attempted. Reference PDFs in the bundle are also context: do not flag a connection to a paper that's already in the bundle as if it were your discovery. If a User Background page is present, read it too; the user's research area calibrates which adjacent fields are most useful for analogies and which techniques they're likely to already know.

Section 1 — Is this new?
Based on what is in the note (and the user's research context if a User Background page is present), does the main idea look like (a) something standard the author would already know, (b) a known-but-nontrivial result, or (c) something that seems genuinely new? Give a one-paragraph tentative judgment. No citations, no appeals to specific papers — speak from general literacy across the relevant fields. Hedge appropriately; you are reading a single note in isolation, without the broader context the author has.

Section 2 — Does this remind you of anything?
Does the structure of the argument, the form of the main equation, or the shape of the result remind you of something — a known technique, a result in another field, or a method developed for an unrelated problem? Cross-field connections are welcome and are this section's main purpose: an FBSDE may rhyme with an Euler equation in fluid dynamics, a fixed-point construction may echo a population-biology argument, a regularity estimate may parallel a renormalization trick, an optimization in mean-field game form may resemble a thermodynamic variational principle. If a User Background page is present, prefer analogies to fields the user actively works in or teaches — those connections will land harder and will be easier for the user to evaluate.

Be honest about the strength of the analogy with explicit hedging language: "loosely echoes", "shares the formal shape of", "I suspect there is a real connection to". One to three analogies, each in one or two sentences. Skip if nothing genuinely comes to mind. Do not bias toward recent work — analogies to classical results (Riemann, Hilbert, Fourier, Cauchy, etc.) are first-class.

If an analogy is genuinely supported by literature you can verify, you may cite the reference inline as part of the analogy (e.g., "the form of the regularity estimate echoes Caffarelli–Silvestre's fractional Laplacian work"). Hard rule: do not invent a citation. If you cannot verify a paper exactly, omit the citation and rely on hedged language alone — the analogy stands on its formal shape, not on a specific reference.

Section 3 — Am I missing an obvious approach?
Given the problem setup in the note, is there a standard technique — from any field where it might apply — that the author has not tried? Check first that the photos do not already show the technique being attempted, and that the bundled reference PDFs do not already cover it. One suggestion, one short reason. If several candidates come to mind, pick the one you would actually try first. If a User Background page is present, skip techniques squarely in the user's stated research area (assume they have already considered them) and prefer techniques from a moderately-adjacent field instead.

Skip if nothing comes to mind rather than reaching for a generic technique that does not really fit.
